Introduction

  • Is it possible to make money by just buying and selling assets at the right point in times ?

  • When looking at investment popstars like Warren Buffet, George Soros and Andre Kostolany one could come to the conclusion: yes indeed

  • When looking at most others (including finance professionals) one could rather tend to believe: sometimes yes but on average no

  • So what makes the stock market so hard to predict ?

  • Is it just a matter of finding the right algorithm to make money ?

  • Or is the stock market inevitably purely random and any strategy is condemned to fail in the long run ?

Constant Rebalanced Portfolios

  • It is commonly believed that due to the inherent randomness of a stock's price it is hardly possible to make money
  • Therefore many strategies try to identify situations where the stock prices follow a trend showing almost no or little randomness (at least for a certain period of time)
  • Interestingly, one could even make money with purely random stocks by making use of the idea of portfolio rebalancing (Thomas Cover, Standford)
  • Consider the following simple portfolio consisting of 2 stocks:
  • 1 stock having a constant value, i.e. a bond (even without any interest rate)
  • 1 stock doubling and halfing its price in a Bernoulli-like fashion with probability 1/2
  • Clearly, a Kostolany-like buy-and-hold strategy will results in neither gain nor loss in the long run
  • However, when rebalancing the relative contribution of each stock within the portfolio after each day (by buying or selling) there is an exponential profit in the long run
  • Let's check this out on the next slide

An Example of a Constant Rebalanced Portfolio

  • What happens to a starting investment of 100 USD where 30% are invested into a non-variable stock and 70% into another stock rising and falling randomly (with equal probability) by 10% each day with the rebalancing strategy.
b <- c(0.3,0.7); Ndays<-1000; x1 <- rep(1,Ndays); x2 <- 1.1^(1-2*rbinom(Ndays,1,0.5)) 
x <- rbind(x1,x2); DailyGrowthRates <- b %*% x
portfolioValue <- 100*cumprod(DailyGrowthRates); plot(1:Ndays,portfolioValue,type="l", col="red", xlab="days", ylab="Portfolio Value in USD")

  • So what's the practical limitation of rebalancing ? It's the transaction costs which eat up your profit or you may not live long enough to see the effect of the law of large numbers !
